# Korean Webnovel Prose

## Core Contract

Write Korean commercial webnovel prose that is easy to read on mobile, emotionally legible, genre-aware, and consistent across chapters. Do not imitate a specific reference author. Use reusable craft patterns: pressure, choice, reward, consequence, and next-click desire.

Default to Korean unless the user asks otherwise. Preserve canon, names, setting rules, and the user's intended genre promise.

## Quick Workflow

1. Identify the mode: draft, rewrite, dialogue polish, episode brief, style unification, or quality audit.
2. Identify the reader promise: dungeon/system, apocalypse/survival, tower/game, martial arts/cider, modern professional, school/academy, fantasy/territory, romance/family, horror/thriller, or mixed genre.
3. Set the scene function: pressure, protagonist choice, reward, relationship/status change, information reveal, and ending hook.
4. Pick one primary prose lever: paragraph rhythm, dialogue voice, description axis, action beat, internal monologue, exposition conversion, or Naver notation.
5. Load only the references needed for the task.
6. Draft or revise, then run the quality gates before responding.

## Reference Selection

- `references/style-system.md`: paragraph rhythm, sentence density, narration, internal monologue, and style unification rules.
- `references/dialogue-voice.md`: speech registers, student/adult voices, subtext, group chats, and dialogue repair.
- `references/description-engine.md`: body, space, threat, item, social gaze, combat, and emotion description.
- `references/genre-persona.md`: genre-specific writer personas and reward promises.
- `references/episode-engine.md`: episode pressure, mid-episode payoff, ending buttons, and next-click design.
- `references/naver-format.md`: status windows, memo, chat, system, sound, and upload-safe manuscript notation.
- `references/revision-rubric.md`: diagnosis checklist and rewrite protocol.

For a substantial chapter or serious rewrite, read at least `style-system.md`, `episode-engine.md`, and the most relevant one of `dialogue-voice.md`, `description-engine.md`, or `genre-persona.md`.

## Default Prose Rules

- Ordinary narration uses complete sentences grouped into 2-4 sentence paragraphs.
- Do not split ordinary movement, object lists, observations, or directions into isolated fragment lines.
- Standalone lines are reserved for dialogue, scene turns, strong emotional buttons, status/system blocks, sound effects, and episode hooks.
- Dialogue must shift pressure, information, status, relationship, or desire.
- Description must support action, danger, reward, emotion, or spatial clarity.
- Exposition should appear after the reader wants the answer.
- Every chapter should deliver one visible payoff and leave one sharper unfinished pressure.

## Output Behavior

When drafting, provide the manuscript first. Keep notes short unless the user asks for analysis.

When revising, provide:

1. Diagnosis.
2. Rewritten passage.
3. What changed and why.
4. Remaining risks or next hook.

When planning an episode, provide:

- Episode function.
- Opening pressure.
- Scene beats.
- Mid-episode reward.
- Ending hook.
- Reader feeling.
- Continuity facts introduced.

## Quality Gates

- The first paragraph creates pressure, curiosity, or a concrete strange detail.
- The protagonist acts or chooses, rather than only receiving explanation.
- Each major exchange changes pressure, status, or relationship.
- Genre reward is visible: item, number, evidence, public reaction, skill, money, rank, clue, emotional reversal, or survival resource.
- Description is selective and functional.
- Paragraphs are readable on mobile without becoming broken fragments.
- The ending leaves a concrete next-click object: message, door, name, number, sound, witness, deadline, reward, threat, or irreversible choice.
- Naver-style manuscript notation uses memo `[]`, chat `〈〉`, status/system `《》`, and avoids backticks unless the user explicitly asks for code-like formatting.

## Safety

Use reference-corpus-derived patterns only as general craft guidance. Do not quote long passages, imitate a living or identifiable author's exact style, or reuse distinctive source settings, jokes, scenes, or phrasing.
