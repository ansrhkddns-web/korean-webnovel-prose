# Reference Reading Methods

## Purpose

This file records craft patterns from direct reading of the local reference corpus. It is not a quotation bank. Do not reproduce source prose, imitate a specific author, or reuse distinctive scenes. Use the patterns as generalized craft tools.

## What The Strong References Actually Do

They do not simply write short sentences. They make every unit perform a job.

- First paragraph: declares pressure, attitude, contradiction, or genre promise.
- Early scene: gives the protagonist a concrete personal cost.
- Dialogue: sounds plain on the surface but moves status underneath.
- Description: selects one actionable detail instead of painting the whole room.
- System/reward: appears as desire, proof, or danger, not as a dry table.
- Ending: leaves a concrete next-click object, not a vague mood.

## Opening Method

A strong commercial opening often uses one of these:

- biased thesis: the narrator states a worldview that reveals wound, humor, or resentment;
- mundane breach: school, work, commute, food, game, phone, or family routine is interrupted;
- system shock: the impossible interface appears before the protagonist understands the world;
- status humiliation: someone higher in the hierarchy insults, ignores, uses, or traps the protagonist;
- sensory aftermath: blood, smell, ruin, silence, fatigue, or pain proves something already happened.

Apply it like this:

1. Start with a line that has attitude or pressure.
2. In 1-3 paragraphs, show what this costs the protagonist.
3. Introduce the genre engine before the reader relaxes.
4. Make the protagonist choose, even if the choice is bad.

## Paragraph Method

Good reference prose uses paragraphs as action packets.

- One paragraph usually contains one flow: observation -> movement -> result, or dialogue -> reaction -> changed pressure.
- Fragment lines are used as buttons, not as default narration.
- Long explanation is broken by action, a question, a message, or a reaction.
- The prose lets the reader understand the spatial/social situation without slowing the scene.

When revising, convert loose fragments into action packets.

## Dialogue Method

Strong dialogue is often ordinary. What makes it work is pressure around the line.

Use a three-layer model:

1. spoken surface: short, natural, age/status-appropriate;
2. hidden motive: fear, contempt, bargaining, avoidance, calculation;
3. external pressure: crowd, chat, superior, enemy, witness, deadline, or status gap.

For first-person schemer or survivor narration, use contrast between polite spoken line and harsher inner thought. For school scenes, keep speech plainer and let group reaction create weight. For adult scenes, let procedure hide threat.

## Description Method

The references usually describe the detail that changes the scene.

- Body detail shows fear, exhaustion, shame, injury, or growth.
- Space detail tells the reader where the next choice is.
- Threat detail makes the protagonist hesitate, hide, attack, or run.
- Item/status detail creates desire or risk.
- Social detail turns a private action into public consequence.

Avoid decorative mood paragraphs unless the mood changes the next action.

## Exposition Method

Rules are strongest after the reader wants them.

- Show failure before naming the rule.
- Show a price before explaining the system.
- Show a public consequence before explaining the hierarchy.
- Let a character misunderstand the rule, then correct it through action.
- Give only the amount of explanation needed for the next decision.

## Reward Method

A reward must be visible and usable.

Weak reward: the protagonist learns abstract lore.

Strong reward:

- gets a skill, item, number, map, witness, contract, rank, food, route, clue, or reputation shift;
- understands what it can do now;
- immediately faces a new cost, watcher, deadline, or temptation.

## Ending Method

Endings work when the next question is concrete.

Use:

- a message or call;
- a named enemy or ally;
- a door, gate, route, floor, dungeon, or stage;
- a changed number or rank;
- a witness;
- a public misunderstanding;
- a reward that reveals a cost;
- the protagonist choosing the dangerous option.

Avoid:

- "something felt ominous" with no object;
- going to sleep;
- preparation with no irreversible action;
- explanation as the final beat.

## Genre-Specific Reading Notes

### Dungeon / System

The strongest scenes move from impossible interface to body-level proof. A message alone is not enough. The protagonist must feel the floor, smell the dungeon, test the item, bleed, or notice the real world changing.

### Apocalypse

The engine is resource plus dread. Food, safety, transport, medicine, shelter, and group trust matter because each can fail. Description should make the reader feel the cost of losing normal life.

### Game / Tower

The engine is rule exploitation. The protagonist wins by understanding a condition faster than others, then the world, audience, ranking, or rival reacts.

### Martial Arts

The engine is hierarchy pressure. Public insult, seniority, sect reputation, restraint, and one clean reversal matter more than long technical explanation.

### Modern Professional

The engine is competence made measurable. Contracts, money, documents, ratings, deals, surgery, broadcast, or promotion should become visible proof.

### School / Academy

The engine is narrow social pressure. A classroom, group chat, teacher, rumor, witness, or test score can become a battlefield. Keep student speech ordinary and let embarrassment or crowd reaction carry the pressure.

### Fantasy / Territory

The engine is cost at scale. Food, troops, roads, taxes, walls, alliances, nobles, and geography must connect to a choice, not sit as background.

## Application Rule

Before drafting, choose:

- opening engine;
- primary pressure;
- protagonist choice;
- visible reward;
- new cost;
- final concrete hook.

If any of these is missing, do not solve it with prettier prose. Fix the scene engine first.
